About me

I am a computational materials scientist currently based at the University of Oxford as a Newton International Fellow, working with Prof. David Manolopoulos FRS in the Department of Chemistry. My research sits at the intersection of condensed matter physics, materials science, and machine learning — with a particular focus on understanding how heat and mass moves through matter at the atomic scale.

I completed my PhD at the University of Hong Kong under Prof. Yue Chen, before holding a Marie Skłodowska-Curie Fellowship at IST Austria with Prof. Bingqing Cheng. Over the years I have worked on topics ranging from ultralow thermal conductivity in crystalline materials and glassy heat transport in perovskites, to water dissociation on oxide surfaces using machine-learning molecular dynamics.

I am passionate about developing and applying machine-learning interatomic potentials to solve problems that traditional methods cannot easily reach — bringing together the accuracy of first-principles theory with the scale of classical simulations.

01

Heat transport in strongly anharmonic materials

Zezhu Zeng, M. Simoncelli, D. E. Manolopoulos
arXiv:2604.19615 (2026)

We introduce a molecular-dynamics route to the phonon spectral function that goes beyond standard perturbation theory. Because classical dynamics reproduces the exact quantum (Kubo-transformed) correlator for harmonic modes, the method extends cleanly to strongly anharmonic crystals — capturing both sharp quasiparticle peaks and broad non-Lorentzian lineshapes, and connecting directly to Wigner heat transport. Benchmarked on PbTe and on the strongly anharmonic halide Cs₃Bi₂I₆Cl₃, it reproduces measured thermal conductivities.

02

Water dissociation on oxide surfaces

Zezhu Zeng, F. Wodaczek, K. Liu, F. Stein, J. Hutter, J. Chen, B. Cheng
Nature Communications 14, 6131 (2023)

Using machine-learning molecular dynamics, we mapped how water splits on seven pristine TiO₂ surfaces — predicting which facets favour dissociation and which keep water intact, and resolving the distinct one-step and two-step proton-transfer pathways behind each. The study shows that the commonly studied anatase (101) and rutile (110) faces alone cannot capture how TiO₂ reacts with water.

03

Glassy heat transport in lattice-distorted crystals

Zezhu Zeng, Z. Fan, M. Simoncelli, C. Chen, T. Liang, Y. Chen, G. Thornton, B. Cheng
Proc. Natl. Acad. Sci. U.S.A. 122, e2415664122 (2025)

Cs₃Bi₂I₆Cl₃ is a crystal, yet it conducts heat almost like a glass. We traced this to intrinsic lattice distortion, which reshuffles the vibrational modes so that heat is carried less by particle-like phonons and more by wave-like tunnelling between modes — the Wigner two-channel regime. This explains how a fully ordered crystal can reach the ultralow, glass-like thermal conductivity usually reserved for amorphous solids.
